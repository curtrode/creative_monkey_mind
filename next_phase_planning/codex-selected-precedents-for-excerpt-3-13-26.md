## Selected Precedents for the Next Phase

The next phase of *Monkey Mind* extends the current stochastic duet toward improvisational, LLM-driven response. This development is being informed by precedent research across electronic literature, computational poetry, and improvisational language systems. The works below are not presented as a comprehensive bibliography, but as a focused context for the project's formal and conceptual evolution.

- **Christopher Strachey, *Love Letters* (1952)**  
  An early model for combinatory text generation and one of the foundational precedents for rule-based literary computation.

- **Alison Knowles and James Tenney, *A House of Dust* (1967)**  
  A landmark generative poem demonstrating how constrained variation can produce scale, atmosphere, and conceptual force.

- **Nick Montfort, *Taroko Gorge* and *Memory Slam***  
  Key precedents for contemporary stochastic text practice and the immediate generative lineage from which *Monkey Mind* departs.

- **Nick Montfort and Stephanie Strickland, *Sea and Spar Between***  
  An important example of combinatory electronic literature operating at poetic and conceptual scale.

- **Jhave Johnston, *ReRites***  
  A major precedent for contemporary AI-assisted poetics and for treating machine generation as an ongoing literary process rather than a novelty.

- **Improbotics (Edinburgh Festival Fringe, 2023)**  
  A useful performance precedent for live AI improvisation, though *Monkey Mind* differs in its literary aims and in its interest in models as distinct voices rather than interchangeable generators.

- **Recent multi-model or multi-agent poetry research**  
  Especially work exploring heterogeneous model ensembles, which helps frame the project's emerging interest in different LLMs as contrasting dramatic voices.

This research will help guide the project's next formal questions: how a stochastic system can begin to "listen," how improvisation can emerge from patterned language, and how different generative systems might be directed as distinct voices within a shared emotional structure.
