# Monkey Mind — Project Proposal

## Lineage

I first encountered Montfort's *Stochastische Texte* reimplementation (of Lutz's 1959 original, translated by Helen MacCormack) while searching for accessible examples of electronic literature to teach in a Digital Creative Writing course. Compared to works like *Taroko Gorge*, the stochastic text engine was immediately legible — easy for students to read, modify, and make their own. It became easy for me to modify, too. As a newer practioner of computational writing myself, I found the engine's simplicity invited experimentation: what happens when you replace Kafka's vocabulary with the language of intimacy — possession, negation, longing, and silence? *Monkey Mind* grew from that question. The project now aims to expand the tradition's canonical forms by introducing dialogue, responsiveness, and emotional arc into a system that has historically operated as monologue.

## Current Work

*Monkey Mind* presents two voices generating text simultaneously, each assembling phrases from relational subjects ("you are my," "I am not your," "we were our") and abstract predicates ("silence," "dream," "darkness," "freedom"). The voices run at different speeds, creating an overlapping texture that evokes two minds thinking past each other — a stochastic duet that never quite resolves into conversation.

The piece uses a terminal-style interface with text-to-speech narration in both synthetic machine and AI-generated human voices. Voices begin muted; the viewer must choose to listen. The title references the Buddhist concept of "monkey mind" — restless consciousness swinging from thought to thought. The subtitle, *Too many tabs open*, grounds this in contemporary digital attention.

## Proposed Development

**Flexible identity.** The fixed "him" and "her" pairing will expand to support any combination — him/him, her/they, they/they — exploring a wider range of relationship dynamics.

**Richer language.** New sentence templates will introduce declarations, questions, negation contrasts, and direct address, expanding the grammar-based generation at the heart of this tradition while keeping the classic Lutz-derived pattern dominant.

**From monologue to dialogue.** The voices will gradually become aware of each other — opening in pure stochastic mode and transitioning into responsive generation where one voice echoes, mirrors, or inverts the other's words. This shift is probabilistic, never fully scripted, tracking the piece's internal rhythm. Crucially, voices can only hear each other through vocalization — unspoken text remains private. Enabling speech transforms parallel monologues into dialogue, making the listener complicit in bridging two minds.

**Emotional arc.** Word pools will evolve through phases mirroring the rhythms of a real relationship: meditation, tension, anger, defensiveness, despair, reconnection, recommitment. The arc is cyclical — ebbing and flowing, revisiting earlier emotional ground at deeper levels.

## Why This Residency

*Monkey Mind* was built on Montfort's reimplementation of Lutz and lives within the computational writing tradition this program represents. The proposed development raises questions that are as much literary as technical: How does dialogue emerge from anxious internal chatter? When does probabilistic language begin to feel like meaning? How do you compose an emotional trajectory for a system that never repeats itself?

These questions are best explored in conversation with practitioners working across grammars, text transformation, simulation, and language models. At the center of this project is a question I don't yet know the answer to: is it even possible to carry the stochastic nature of the original into something truly dialogic, in real time? With access to LLMs and their more natural capacities for language, I now have tools that might make this feasible — models that can parse, transform, and respond to a partner's output with greater sensitivity than rule-based approaches alone. The tension between the piece's stochastic roots and these newer capabilities — between Lutz's random selection and a model that approximates understanding — is the creative space I want to work in. The self-directed structure of the residency aligns with how this project develops: through iterative cycles of writing, listening, and tuning until the output surprises its author.
